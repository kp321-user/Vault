**1. Chronological.** The default: Pre-Socratics → Plato/Aristotle → Hellenistic → medieval → early modern → Kant → 19th century → 20th century. Russell's _History_ is this, with his signature addition of social context (each philosophy as a product of its political moment). Strengths: you see influence flowing forward, and nothing is anachronistic — you understand why Descartes obsesses over certainty once you've seen the Reformation shatter the medieval consensus. Weakness: early material feels remote before you know why it matters, and the thread implies more continuity than exists.

**2. Problem-based.** Organize by the standing questions: What can I know? (epistemology) What exists? (metaphysics) How should I act? (ethics) What makes a society just? (political philosophy) What is the mind? What is beauty? Each problem has its own timeline. This is how philosophy is actually taught in most universities now, and it fits how philosophers see themselves — as working on problems, not eras. The classic problems are remarkably few: maybe a dozen big ones, each with a 2,500-year argument attached. Weakness: you lose the sense that Plato's epistemology and his politics are one system; problems get artificially quarantined from each other.

**3. Concept genealogies.** Trace a single concept's mutations across time — exactly what you've been doing by accident. Your doubt/certainty thread is one: Socratic ignorance → Academic skepticism → Cicero → Augustine → Montaigne → Descartes → Hume → Kant → Peirce/Dewey. Others with equally clean arcs: _the will_ (Aristotle's lack of the concept → Augustine's invention of it → Aquinas → Schopenhauer → Nietzsche), _the self_, _natural rights_, _substance_, _happiness/eudaimonia_, _evil_ (privation theory onward — another one you've already started). This is the Nietzsche method, literally: the _Genealogy_ is a concept-genealogy of "good." It produces the most vivid understanding per hour invested, but assumes some chronological scaffolding already exists, or you can't place the mutations.

**4. Dialectical chains — thinker-responds-to-thinker.** Philosophy as a 2,500-year argument where each major figure is answering a predecessor: Aristotle contra Plato, Augustine contra Pelagius and the Academics, Locke contra Descartes, Hume wakes Kant from "dogmatic slumber," Kierkegaard and Marx both contra Hegel, Nietzsche contra basically everyone but especially Christianity-Platonism ("Christianity is Platonism for the people" is his line). Reading paired antagonists — as you've been doing with Augustine/Nietzsche — is probably the highest-engagement approach, because opposition makes each position sharp. A survey built purely this way looks like a tournament bracket rather than a timeline.

**5. Schools and traditions.** Stoics, Epicureans, Skeptics; Thomists; rationalists vs. empiricists; German idealism; pragmatism; phenomenology and existentialism; analytic philosophy. Useful because thinkers really did work in schools with shared premises, and the school label compresses a lot. The rationalist/empiricist vs. Kant story is the standard narrative spine of early modern philosophy. Caution: some school groupings are retrospective inventions that flatten differences (the "rationalist/empiricist" split itself is partly a Kantian propaganda victory).

**6. The hinge-points thread.** Organize around a handful of ruptures where the whole enterprise changed shape: the Socratic turn (from cosmos to ethics), the Christian synthesis (Athens meets Jerusalem — Augustine is the hinge), the scientific revolution (Descartes/Bacon — knowledge becomes method), the Kantian "Copernican revolution" (from what the world is to what the mind contributes), the linguistic turn (20th century — from ideas to language), and arguably the Darwinian turn Dewey rode (mind as biological instrument, which is exactly the premise of the chapter you just converted). Six or seven hinges give you a skeleton you can hang everything else on.

**7. Reception and transmission history.** How ideas physically survived and traveled: what was lost (most of the Presocratics, Cicero's _Hortensius_), who preserved what (Boethius, the Arabic translators — Avicenna and Averroes are why Europe got Aristotle back), which translations mattered, how printing and universities changed the game. This is the materialist thread — less about arguments than about pipes and bottlenecks. Underrated, and it connects philosophy to the general history you're already building for HUB.

**8. Biographical/lives-based.** Philosophy through the lived situations that produced it — Socrates' trial, Boethius writing in prison, Spinoza's excommunication, Hume's cheerfulness, Nietzsche's collapse. The ancient view (Hadot's _Philosophy as a Way of Life_) held that a philosophy _was_ a life-practice, not a doctrine set; the biographical thread recovers that. Your Cicero→Augustine question was this lens: the life as the key to the output.

For the vault specifically, the practical answer is that Russell already gives you thread 1 as the spine, and your existing notes are already threads 3 and 4 (concept genealogies plus antagonist pairs). The move that makes a vault outperform any single book is deliberately running the cross-links: a chronological philosopher stub for Augustine, linked to concept notes (doubt, evil-as-privation, the will, grace) and to antagonist notes (contra Pelagius, contra the Academics, Nietzsche contra Augustine). Each new reading then just adds nodes and edges. The one thread I'd suggest adding consciously is #6 — a short "hinges" MOC — because it's the cheapest way to keep the whole map legible as it grows.

One honest caveat: any survey thread quietly encodes a thesis. Russell's chronology argues that philosophy progresses toward (his) empiricism; Hegel's history argues everything culminates in Hegel; problem-based surveys imply the problems are eternal rather than historically manufactured — which Dewey explicitly denies in the book you're now reading; his claim is that the whole theory/practice problem is a cultural artifact, not a permanent feature. So whichever thread you pick, it's worth a vault note on what the thread itself is smuggling in.
