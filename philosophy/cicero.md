# Cicero (106–43 BC)
## In plain terms
Cicero was a famous Roman orator and translator of Greek to Latin. His skeptical "Academic" (Plato's school in Athens) works shaped Augustine's skeptical phase. _De Republica_ supplied the definition of a commonwealth — a people united by common acknowledgment of right and common interest — that Augustine famously interrogates and revises in _City of God_ XIX, arguing that by Cicero's own definition Rome never was a true republic, since there is no true right without justice, and no justice where God isn't given his due. Cicero ignites Augustine; a millennium later Petrarch's rediscovery of Cicero's letters helps ignite the Renaissance — and Petrarch carried Augustine's _Confessions_ in his pocket. The two keep converting people in tandem.

He was also a working politician, not just a writer: as consul in 63 BC he exposed and crushed the Catilinarian conspiracy, the event Sallust later made the case study for Roman moral decline. His four _Catilinarian Orations_ survive — delivered during the crisis, but polished for publication years later, so even the "live" source is a curated rebroadcast. He ended on the losing side of the civil wars and was executed in the proscriptions of 43 BC.

## Influences on Cicero
- Greek philosophy generally — his project was translating and adapting it for a Roman audience
- [[the-academy]] (Academic skepticism — Philo of Larissa was his teacher)
- [[Plato]] (_De Republica_ and _De Legibus_ are modeled on Plato's _Republic_ and _Laws_)

## Who Cicero influenced
- [[Augustine]] — twice over: the lost _Hortensius_ triggered his turn to philosophy (_Confessions_ III), and _De Republica_ gave _City of God_ XIX its target definition
- Petrarch and Renaissance humanism (rediscovery of the letters, 1345)
- Descartes' skeptical method, indirectly, via the Academic skepticism Cicero transmitted (the same current behind _si fallor sum_)

## Key works
- _Catilinarian Orations_ (63 BC, published c. 60) — the conspiracy from the accuser's side; read against [[Sallust]]'s account written twenty years later
- _De Republica_ (54–51 BC) — source of the commonwealth definition; survives only in fragments, ironically partly recovered from a palimpsest
- _Hortensius_ (45 BC) — lost; known mainly through Augustine's testimony that it converted him to philosophy at nineteen
- _Academica_ (45 BC) — Academic skepticism; background to Augustine's _Contra Academicos_
- _De Officiis_ (44 BC) — Stoic-derived ethics of duty; his most influential work in later centuries
- Letters (to Atticus, to friends) — the trove Petrarch found

## Connected problems
- Can a definition condemn its own object? (Augustine's _CoG_ XIX move: accept Cicero's definition, conclude Rome never qualified — same structure as "a people is what it loves")
- Source asymmetry: nearly everything on Catiline traces to Cicero (maximally interested) and Sallust (moralist with a thesis); no account from Catiline's side survives
