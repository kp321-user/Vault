# Augustine (354–430 AD)

## In plain terms
Augustine became a monastic scholar and church father who's early life was emotional and undisciplined. He became a follower of Mani, a religion that preached a clear all-encompassing view of life: good vs evil as battling forces. He left Manichaeism after seeing its limits explaining current astronomical discoveries. Greek skepticism eroded his confidence in the Manichee's false-certainty.

After applying a skeptical lens to his world-view, he left Manicheism for Christianity. There he continued to live as a rhetorician and scholar, and eventually is promoted to bishop of Hippo. His autobiography Confessions describes his journey (real and intellectual) and is written as a discussion with God. He explores philosophy and Christian theology in a introspective manner that is disarming and deep. 

He is alive during the first sacking of Rome by the Visigoths in 410. Roman pagans partly blame the sacking and resulting disillusionment on Rome's conversion to Christianity. Augustine spent 13 years writing a reply in the form of _The City of God_, declaring its message to be spiritual rather than political: Christianity should be concerned with the mystical Heavenly City, rather than with the politics of the Earthly City.

**Structure.** The work divides into two parts: Books I–X are a polemic against Roman paganism, first attacking pagan religion as unable to secure even worldly benefits (I–V), then attacking pagan philosophy—especially the Platonists—as unable to secure eternal life (VI–X). Books XI–XXII develop Augustine's own account of the two cities across three movements: their origins in the angelic fall and Adam's sin (XI–XIV), their parallel histories from Cain and Abel through Christ (XV–XVIII), and their final destinies at the Last Judgment—damnation for the earthly city, resurrection and eternal happiness for the heavenly (XIX–XXII). The organizing thread throughout is the contrast between a city built on love of self and one built on love of God.

### Core ideas

1. **Evil as privation.** Not a substance but an absence of good — his answer to the Manichaean dualism he once believed. Solves a real logical problem for monotheism: no second power needed to explain evil.
2. **The divided will.** Wanting to want something, and failing — genuinely original moral psychology (_Confessions_ VIII), still cited. See [[divided-will]].
3. **Doubt as foundation.** _Si fallor, sum_ — even if I am deceived, I exist. His reply to Academic skepticism, anticipating Descartes by twelve centuries (_Contra Academicos_, _City of God_ XI).
4. **Time as distension of the mind.** Past and future exist only as present memory and present expectation (_Confessions_ XI) — the one stretch of Augustine analytic philosophers still argue about.
5. **The mind as image of the Trinity.** Memory, understanding, and will as three faculties of one mind — his psychological model for the three-persons-one-God problem (_De Trinitate_).
6. **Grace vs. Pelagius.** Can humans choose good unaided, or is salvation entirely God's gift? This dispute structures the Reformation a millennium later.
7. **The two cities.** Earthly and heavenly, defined not by borders but by their loves — self-love versus love of God. Founds Christian political theology.
8. **A people is what it loves.** Groups have shared character but no group soul — "Rome has no soul to save," so its fall is a calamity, not a theological catastrophe. Rome's defining love is _libido dominandi_, the lust for domination, named in the preface of _City of God_ — and the phrase cuts both ways: the dominator is enslaved by the drive to dominate. Roman virtue was real but grew out of a cultivated love of praise; pride doesn't stop being pride because it produces impressive results. Unlike the modern, neutral sense of "culture," Augustine's account is evaluative through and through: a shared love can be a shared distortion — self-love scaled up, socially reinforced, and laundered by the group.
9. **No improving people in bulk.** Moral improvement is a change in what a person loves, and loves live only in individuals — a thousand conversions, never one mass conversion. Structure and force can suppress vice (worth doing) but cannot reorder anyone's loves. Collective arrangements are real levers, but they work by arranging the _conditions_ of individual change, never by acting on the group as such.

## Influences on Augustine
- [[plato]]
- [[plotinus]]  
- Cicero (106–43 BC) famous Roman orator and translator of Greek to Latin. His skeptical  "Academic" (Plato's school in Athens) works shaped Augustine's skeptical phase. _De Republica_ supplied the definition of a commonwealth — a people united by common acknowledgment of right and common interest — that Augustine famously interrogates and revises in _City of God_ XIX, arguing that by Cicero's own definition Rome never was a true republic, since there is no true right without justice, and no justice where God isn't given his due. Cicero ignites Augustine; a millennium later Petrarch's rediscovery of Cicero's letters helps ignite the Renaissance — and Petrarch carried Augustine's _Confessions_ in his pocket. The two keep converting people in tandem.

## Key works
- **Confessions (c. 397–400)** — the autobiographical masterpiece, narrating his life to his conversion and mother's death, then turning to memory, time, and a reading of Genesis. https://www.ccel.org/ccel/augustine/confessions/confessions.i.html
- **City of God (413–426)** — the 22-book magnum opus, occasioned by the sack of Rome; the two cities, the critique of Rome, the theology of history. Meaning and moral reality live at the level of the individual heart's orientation, which is invisible, slow to change, and cross-cuts every group — so hope directed at collectives, nations, and worldly outcomes is misplaced, while the suspicion you should reserve for any group claiming to redeem you cannot be too strong.
- **On the Trinity (c. 400–428)** — roughly two decades of work on the doctrine and the psychological analogies (memory-understanding-will); the deep source of his analysis of mind.
