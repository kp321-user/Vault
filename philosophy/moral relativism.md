Methodological relativism — suspend judgment in order to describe accurately — is a claim about method. It quietly gets upgraded, often without anyone noticing, into moral relativism — there is no fact about whether a practice is better or worse. That's a claim about reality. The first is a discipline's working rule; the second is a contested philosophical thesis. The definition gives you the first for free. It does not give you the second, and treating the neutral lens as evidence for moral relativism is a category error. So, removing Augustine: can we claim cultures are morally neutral?
Only if you smuggle. Three positions, and notice none of them is settled by the anthropological definition:

- **The practices themselves.** Widow-burning, chattel slavery, footbinding, human sacrifice, honor killing. To say these are _morally neutral_ is not a neutral observation — it's a strong and unusual ethical claim, and almost nobody actually holds it when pressed. The neutral _description_ ("practice X exists in society Y and functions thus") does not entail a neutral _evaluation_. Conflating the two is the core error.
- **The moral-realist answer.** If any moral facts exist at all — if cruelty really is worse than kindness, not merely disapproved-of — then cultures embodying more cruelty are worse, full stop, and neutrality is false about the objects even though it remains a fine rule for the describer. Most people are closet realists here: they'll defend relativism in the abstract and then judge the Aztecs.
- **The relativist answer.** If moral facts are themselves cultural products — if "wrong" just means "proscribed by some culture" — then there's no external stand-point from which to rank cultures, and neutrality holds. But this is a _substantive metaethical thesis_ you'd have to argue for, and it has famous costs: it makes moral reformers (abolitionists, dissidents) definitionally wrong-by-their-own-culture's-lights, and it makes cross-cultural condemnation incoherent. Defensible, but not free, and not implied by anthropology's method.
