# Alfred North Whitehead (1861–1947)

## In plain terms

Modern science since Descartes and Newton built its success on one powerful
abstraction: matter moving through measurable space and time. Whitehead's
argument is that somewhere along the way this abstraction got mistaken for
the whole of reality. What gets left out is everything that makes reality
actually alive — experience, process, value, relationship, creativity. He
wasn't anti-science; he was a mathematician. His critique was that
scientific materialism had overreached its own method and was making
metaphysical claims it wasn't entitled to make.

His alternative: reality is not made of static substances (things) but of
events and processes (occasions of experience). Nothing simply *is* — 
everything is *becoming*. Each moment of experience absorbs what came before
it, responds creatively, and flows into what comes next. This applies not
just to human consciousness but to all of nature.

---

## Core ideas

**Fallacy of misplaced concreteness** — mistaking an abstraction for
concrete reality. The classic example: Newtonian matter is a useful
scientific model, but treating it as the fundamental stuff of the universe
elevates a map to the status of territory.

**Process over substance** — reality is better understood as a succession
of events (occasions of experience) than as persistent things. No entity
exists in isolation; everything is constituted by its relationships to
everything that preceded it.

**Prehension** — Whitehead's term for how each occasion of experience
absorbs and responds to prior occasions. Not quite perception (which implies
a conscious subject), but a more general sensitivity that applies at every
level of nature.

**Eternal objects** — aspects of reality that are outside time, akin to
[[plato|Plato's Forms]]. Where Whitehead diverges from pure process: some
patterns and possibilities are permanent, not becoming.

**Philosophy of organism** — his term for his own system. Reality is better
modelled on a living organism than on a machine. Every entity has something
like interiority, however minimal.

**Science, aesthetics, ethics, religion** — Whitehead argued in *Science
and the Modern World* that these four modes of human intuition each make a
genuine contribution to a community's worldview. When any one dominates
completely — especially science — the picture of reality becomes distorted.

## Influences on Whitehead

- [[plato]] — eternal objects echo the Theory of Forms; Whitehead called
  European philosophy "a series of footnotes to Plato"
- [[heraclitus]] — constant flux as the nature of reality; nothing static
- [[kant]] — the structure of experience; categories that shape how reality
  is encountered
- [[william-james]] — radical empiricism; experience as the starting point,
  not matter
- [[bergson]] — time as lived duration; vitalism; intuition over
  abstraction; the most direct influence on process philosophy
- [[leibniz]] — monads as self-contained units of experience; Whitehead
  secularized and interconnected this idea
- [[hegel]] — process and reality as dynamic rather than fixed; though
  Whitehead rejected Hegel's dialectical determinism
- [[dewey]] — pragmatism; knowledge as embedded in experience and process

---

## Who Whitehead influenced

- [[bergson]] — mutual; the two were in conversation
- Gilles Deleuze — process ontology, becoming over being
- Bruno Latour — actor-network theory; non-human agency
- Charles Hartshorne — process theology
- Ecology and environmental philosophy — organism model over machine model

---

## Key works

- *Science and the Modern World* (1925) — most accessible; the critique of
  scientific materialism
- *Process and Reality* (1929) — the full system; notoriously dense
- *Adventures of Ideas* (1933) — more readable; civilization and ideas
