# # Thucydides (c. 460–c. 400 BC)
## In plain terms
Athenian general turned historian of the Peloponnesian War — exiled after a military failure, which freed him to observe both sides. He founded explanation without the gods: events have discoverable causes beneath their official justifications, and his distinction between pretext and "truest cause" (Sparta's fear of rising Athenian power) is arguably the first of its kind in Western thought. He claimed his work was a "possession for all time" because human nature is constant — under similar pressures, similar results. In the Corcyra civil war chapters he catches moral vocabulary changing under pressure — reckless daring rebranded as courage, moderation as weakness — the primal scene of genealogical critique. The chain runs Thucydides → Sallust → Augustine and (much later) Nietzsche: one technique, transmitted — read moral language symptomatically. What he refused is as defining as what he did: no providence, no progress, no direction. History as recurrence, not story. That refusal marks the line Augustine crosses.

---

## Core ideas
- Pretext vs. truest cause (alēthestatē prophasis)
- Constant human nature as the ground of historical knowledge
- Moral language under pressure (Corcyra, Book 3)
- Power over justice between unequals (Melian Dialogue, Book 5)
- History as recurrence — no telos

## Influences on Thucydides
- Herodotus (as foil — inquiry inherited, divine causation dropped)
- Hippocratic medicine (diagnosis, prognosis, observation — a common scholarly view, not his own statement)
- 
## Who Thucydides influenced
- [[Sallust]] — conscious stylistic and methodological model; *metus hostilis* is a Thucydidean cause transplanted to Rome
- [[Augustine]] — indirectly, through Sallust's decline narrative as *City of God*'s scaffold
- Hobbes — translated him (1629), called him "the most politick historiographer that ever writ"; conduit into political realism
- Machiavelli and modern realism — via the constant-human-nature premise
- [[Nietzsche]] — praised him in *Twilight of the Idols* as an antidote to Plato ("cure" / "recovery" language; verify wording)
- 
## Key works
- *History of the Peloponnesian War* (unfinished, breaks off 411 BC) — the only work. Key sites: Book 1 (method, truest cause), Book 2 (Pericles' funeral oration, the plague), Book 3 (Corcyra), Book 5 (Melian Dialogue), Books 6–7 (Sicilian expedition)

## Personal relevance
discussion copied:
my question: "how does Thucydides fit into the philosophical history?

Claude answer:
"Thucydides is where the argument for "history as a vehicle for philosophy" really starts, and his position in the lineage is defined by three moves he made and one he refused.

The first move: he stripped the gods out of historical explanation. Herodotus, a generation earlier, still lets divine retribution and oracles do causal work. Thucydides explains the Peloponnesian War entirely through human material — fear, honor, interest, power. His famous distinction between the stated grievances and the "truest cause" (_alēthestatē prophasis_) of the war — Sparta's fear of rising Athenian power — is arguably the first explicit separation of pretext from underlying cause in Western thought. That's a philosophical act disguised as narration: it asserts that events have discoverable structural causes beneath their official justifications. Sallust's whole method descends from this, and you can see the family resemblance in the _metus hostilis_ thesis — fear as the hidden variable — which is essentially a Thucydidean cause transplanted to Rome.

The second move: he claimed his work would be useful because human nature is constant. His stated purpose is to serve those who want to understand what happened and what will, "in accordance with human nature," happen again in similar form — the work as a "possession for all time" rather than a performance piece. This is a philosophical anthropology smuggled in as a methods statement: if _anthrōpeia physis_ is stable, then history yields general knowledge, not just stories. Every later attempt to extract laws or patterns from history — Machiavelli explicitly, modern political realism explicitly — leans on this premise.

The third move, and the one that plugs most directly into your genealogy thread: the analysis of moral language under pressure. In the description of the civil war at Corcyra, Thucydides observes that words changed their accustomed meanings — reckless daring got redescribed as courage, prudent hesitation as cowardice, moderation as weakness. That is the primal scene of genealogical critique: moral vocabulary exposed as a function of power and faction rather than a neutral description of virtue. Sallust's "Rome praised her own crime and called it glory" is that Corcyra insight applied to an empire; Augustine's unmasking of Roman virtue as splendid vice is the next iteration; Nietzsche's genealogy is the same operation with the polarity reversed. So the chain Harding sketches — Thucydides → Sallust → both Augustine and Nietzsche — is at bottom the transmission of one technique: read moral language symptomatically. Add the Melian Dialogue, where Athenian envoys tell a doomed neutral island that justice only exists between equals in power and the strong do what they can while the weak suffer what they must, and you have the founding text of political realism as well — Hobbes, who made the first great English translation of Thucydides and called him "the most politick historiographer that ever writ," is the direct conduit into modern political philosophy.

Now the refusal, which is what separates him from philosophy of history in the strict sense: Thucydides has no direction. No providence, no progress, no decline-toward-an-end — just recurrence, because constant human nature under similar pressures produces similar results. Philosophers of history in the technical sense (Löwith's _Meaning in History_ is the classic statement of this view) tend to date the field's real beginning to Augustine, precisely because philosophy of history requires a _telos_ — a claim about where the whole story is going — and that requires the linear, eschatological time Christianity introduced. On this reading the lineage splits cleanly: Thucydides founds the _science_ of history (causes, patterns, human nature), Augustine founds the _philosophy_ of history (meaning, direction, judgment), and Sallust is the hinge between them — Thucydidean method, moralized, handed to Augustine, who bolts it onto a theology of time.

Which gives your Vault a tidy three-way contrast if you want it: Thucydides, history without judgment or direction; Sallust, history with judgment but no direction (decline, but no destination); Augustine, history with both — every people judged by its loves, and the whole story running toward the two cities' final separation. Each figure adds one layer, and the layer Augustine adds is exactly the one your "a people is what it loves" note is about.

Confidence flags: the Corcyra passage (Book 3) and Melian Dialogue (Book 5) are bedrock, high confidence, though my renderings are paraphrase — check exact wording against a translation before quoting. The Hobbes quote is well-attested but verify wording. Löwith's thesis is accurately characterized from memory; the attribution of "philosophy of history begins with Augustine" is a scholarly position, not a consensus fact — Vico, Hegel, or Herodotus partisans would contest it."

## Connected problems
- Can history yield knowledge, or only stories? (His answer: knowledge, iff human nature is constant — the premise everything realist rests on)
- Where does philosophy of history begin? Löwith's position: not here — it needs a telos, so it begins with [[Augustine]]. Thucydides founds the science of history; Augustine the philosophy of it; [[Sallust]] is the hinge
- Three-way contrast: Thucydides = judgment-free, direction-free; Sallust = judgment, no direction; Augustine = both ("a people is what it loves" is the judgment layer)
