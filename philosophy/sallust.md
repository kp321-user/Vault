# Sallust (86–35 BC)
## In plain terms
Roman historian, ex-politician (expelled from the Senate, corruption cloud over his governorship), Caesarian partisan — a compromised man who wrote the most influential moral diagnosis of Rome ever produced. His *Bellum Catilinae* frames a failed coup as a symptom: Rome stayed virtuous only while Carthage stood, because fear of the enemy (*metus hostilis*) held the city together; remove the rival, and prosperity rots into ambition, greed, and men like Catiline. His signature phrase, *lubido dominandi* — the lust for mastery as a cause of war — named a vice of foreign empires: Cyrus, Sparta, Athens. Augustine's masterstroke was to steal the phrase and turn it on Rome itself (*City of God*, preface and III.14), collapsing the distinction Sallust used to spare his own city. One technique runs through him: virtue-language read as a mask — "Rome praised her own crime and called it glory." Thucydides taught him the move; Augustine and Nietzsche each inherited it, one to baptize it, one to weaponize it against baptism.

---

## Core ideas
- *Metus hostilis* — external fear as social glue; its removal (Carthage, 146 BC) as the hinge of Roman decline
- *Lubido dominandi* — the lust for mastery as cause of war; the lust dominates the one it possesses (cf. *BC* 51: "si lubido possidet, ea dominatur")
- Virtue-language as mask — crime praised as glory; genealogy of morals before the name
- Style as argument — systematic archaisms (Cato the Elder's diction) performing the ancestral severity Rome lost

## Influences on Sallust
- [[Thucydides]] — conscious model: compressed style, paired speeches, causes beneath pretexts; naming Athens at *BC* 2.2 is practically a citation
- Cato the Elder — vocabulary mined so openly a contemporary called him a thief of Cato's words
- 
## Who Sallust influenced
- [[Augustine]] — his most-quoted historian in *City of God*; supplied both the decline narrative and the *libido dominandi* apparatus, which Augustine radicalized: not decline from real virtue, but a flaw from the founding
- [[Nietzsche]] — named him in *Twilight of the Idols* ("What I Owe to the Ancients") as the model of his style: compression, severity, maximum substance
- Tacitus — his stylistic and moral heir among Roman historians
- Machiavelli and early modern republicanism — the vocabulary of corruption and *virtù* (via the *Discourses*; Cambridge-school territory)
- 
## Key works
- *Bellum Catilinae* (c. 42 BC) — the conspiracy of 63 BC as case study in decline; preface (ch. 1–13) carries the philosophy, Caesar–Cato debate (ch. 50–53) is the set piece, Catiline dies well for a rotten cause (ch. 60–61)
- *Bellum Jugurthinum* — war against the Numidian king; Roman corruption abroad
- *Histories* — covered the 70s BC; survives only in fragments
- Translations: Woodman (Penguin, 2007) for fidelity to the rough style; Batstone (Oxford, 2010) for polish; Ramsey's Loeb (2013) for facing Latin

## Connected problems
- The compromised moralist: does Sallust's own corruption undercut the diagnosis, or is he evidence for it?
- Sallust vs. [[Cicero]] on 63 BC: the two main sources pull opposite ways — Cicero inflates his role, Sallust deflates it (the great-man pairing is Caesar and Cato, not Cicero); truth between two motivated narrators
- Moralizing or philosophy? Never asks what virtue *is*, never defends his causal claims — yet the same objection would exclude Thucydides. Carrier of ideas philosophy later ran with
- The Sallust fork: [[Augustine]] and [[Nietzsche]] as rival heirs of one genealogical technique (Harding, *Augustine and Roman Virtue*, Continuum 2008)
- Three-way contrast with [[Thucydides]] and [[Augustine]]: no judgment/no direction → judgment, no direction → both
