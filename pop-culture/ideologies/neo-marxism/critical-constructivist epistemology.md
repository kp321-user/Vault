## Core idea

The central claim is that knowledge is not neutral or purely objective; it is made through social processes and filtered by culture, history, and ideology. In Kincheloe’s framing, this means researchers and teachers should ask how “truth” gets constructed and how dominant groups influence what counts as valid knowledge.[peterlang](https://www.peterlang.com/document/1100363)

## Why it matters

Kincheloe treats critical constructivism as both an epistemology and a critique of power, so it is not just about learning theory but about who gets to define reality in education and research. The approach is meant to help students and educators recognize hidden assumptions in textbooks, institutions, and scientific claims.[peterlang](https://www.peterlang.com/document/1100363)

## In one sentence

If you’re looking for a compact definition, **critical constructivist epistemology** in Kincheloe’s 2005 work is the view that knowledge is socially produced, historically situated, and shaped by power relations.
