You're reaching toward a real model — but here's the delicious irony: the model you just described is _Pelagius's_ psychology, and Augustine spent twenty years arguing it doesn't work. You've independently reconstructed the position he built his whole anthropology against.

Your model runs: intellect perceives the value of a trait → decides to adopt it → adopts it, effortfully, against temperament. That's intellect as executive — see the good, install the good. It's also basically Socrates' view (nobody errs knowingly; virtue is knowledge) and Aristotle's, softened (you acquire virtues by deliberate practice, becoming just by doing just acts). It's intuitive, flattering to the intellect, and half of modern self-improvement culture assumes it.

Augustine's report from inside his own case is that it failed at the decisive step. Look at the timeline: he _saw_ the value of humility years before he had any. He'd read it, admired it in Ambrose, was pierced by the stories of Antony and the monks — men with none of his education who simply _did_ what he could only endorse. Book VIII's agony is precisely the gap between perceiving a value and being able to adopt it: "the mind commands the mind to will, and yet it does not." His conclusion, which we walked through earlier with grace: the intellect can diagnose but cannot install, because traits live in what you _love_, and love is not under direct voluntary control. You can't decide to find humility delicious the way you can't decide to find someone attractive. Seeing that humility is valuable is an intellectual event; _wanting_ it more than you want admiration is a change of appetite — and appetite, on his account, has to be changed _in_ you, not _by_ you. That's what he means by grace: the flip happened to him in the garden, not through resolve.

But — and this rescues most of your model — the intellect and effort aren't idle; they're just demoted from engine to maintenance crew. After the flip, everything Augustine did looks like deliberate trait-engineering of exactly the kind you're describing: he built a rule-governed community (environment design), submitted to daily communal correction, carved that anti-gossip warning into his own dinner table (a literal behavioral prompt), and ran the perpetual self-audit of Book X, watching himself enjoy praise and flagging it in real time. That's the old habit-chain mechanism from Book VIII deliberately run in reverse — link by link, custom becoming second nature, but in the good direction this time. So the full Augustinian mechanism is: grace reorders the love; practice consolidates the trait; intellect supplies vigilance. Your model keeps steps two and three and disputes only step one.

Interestingly, modern psychology lands somewhere between you and him. Deliberate trait change is real but weak when attempted by raw resolution — willpower-first approaches fail roughly the way Augustine's pre-conversion resolutions did — and much stronger when routed through environment, social structure, and identity shifts, which is the monastery model. And the initiating event in documented big personality changes is very often something that _happens to_ the person — a relationship, a crisis, a conversion experience — rather than a decision. Augustine would nod: that's grace, naturalized.

So: you're right that his humility was learned and counter-temperamental rather than native, and right that intellect played a role. Where he'd stop you is at "deciding to adopt." His claim, from the inside, is that he tried exactly that for years and it produced only the wish to be humble — which, he'd note dryly, is itself often just pride in better clothing. The trait came when the wanting changed, and the wanting changed from outside the will. Whether you buy the theological frame or translate it into secular terms (identity shift, environmental scaffolding, the limits of executive control), the psychological observation underneath has held up remarkably well for sixteen centuries.

Synthesized philosophical lineage from Schopenhauer through Frankfurt on volition's limits

The claim has three layers worth pulling apart: a logical structure, a phenomenological observation, and then the empirical record. Let me take them in order.

**The logical structure: the regress problem.** Start with the distinction the philosopher Harry Frankfurt made famous in the 1970s, though Augustine effectively had it: first-order desires (I want admiration) versus second-order desires (I want to _not_ want admiration; I want to want humility). Your intellect operates at the second level — it can survey your first-order wants and rank them, endorse some, repudiate others. The question is whether that second-order judgment has any _causal power_ over the first-order wants. And here's the trap: suppose you try to will a new want into existence. With what do you will it? With your existing motivational set — which is precisely the thing you're trying to change. Every act of will is powered by some desire already in place; the will has no fuel of its own. So attempting to will a change in your wants is like trying to lift the chair you're sitting on. You can will _actions_ against your desires (grit your teeth, decline the praise), but the desire sits there undestroyed, and gritted teeth is exactly the state Augustine describes as the divided will — behavior changed, wanting intact, the whole arrangement unstable and exhausting. Schopenhauer compressed this into the most famous one-liner on the subject: "Man can do what he wills, but he cannot will what he wills." Nietzsche, from the opposite camp theologically, agreed completely — he thought conscious willing was a surface effect of drives it doesn't control. So Augustine, Schopenhauer, and Nietzsche — a Christian saint, a pessimist atheist, and the Antichrist's author — all converge on the same structural point. When enemies agree, it's usually because they're looking at the same fact.

**The phenomenology: what change actually feels like from inside.** Notice how people who have undergone deep motivational change describe it. Almost never: "I decided, and then by increments of effort I came to want differently." Almost always: language of _being changed_ — something lifted, broke, shifted, came over me; I fell out of love; the desire just wasn't there anymore; I saw it differently and couldn't unsee it. William James collected exactly these reports in _The Varieties of Religious Experience_ (1902) — the direct scientific descendant of _Confessions_ Book VIII, and James knew it; Augustine is all over that book. James's finding was that transformative change is typically experienced as _received_, arriving suddenly or as the release of a long subconscious incubation, often precisely when effortful striving is abandoned — surrender preceding the shift. He was agnostic about theology but firm about the psychology: the conscious will is not where the action is; it's more like the last to find out. A century later the psychologist William Miller (of motivational interviewing fame) studied what he called "quantum change" — people whose values and desires reorganized rapidly and durably — and found the same signature: unbidden, surprising, frequently following despair rather than resolve, and stable for decades afterward. The garden scene, replicated under survey conditions.

**The empirical record.** Several independent research lines have converged on the Augustinian shape:

_Addiction_ is the cleanest case, because it's motivation disorder in pure form. Decades of evidence show that wanting to quit — sincere, informed, intelligent wanting-to-want — predicts recovery only weakly; white-knuckle willpower has dismal long-term rates. What works better: changed environments, changed social ties, changed identity ("I am a non-smoker" outperforms "I am trying not to smoke"), and — awkwardly for secular tastes — surrender-based programs. AA's first step is literally the admission that the will is powerless over the want, and its mechanism is community plus a "higher power" however understood: Augustine's monastery and grace, operationalized. Neuroscience added a mechanistic footnote with Kent Berridge's dissociation of "wanting" from "liking" — incentive salience runs on subcortical dopamine circuitry that cognition can observe but not directly command, which is about as literal a vindication of "the intellect diagnoses but cannot install" as biology could supply.

_Personality research_ spent mid-century assuming traits were fixed, then discovered they do change — but look at what drives it. The biggest documented shifts come from role and relationship transitions (first job, partnership, parenthood — "social investment theory"), i.e., from life happening to people. Deliberate volitional change has now been demonstrated (Nathan Hudson's studies: people can move their Big Five scores on purpose), but with a crucial catch — it works only when the person executes concrete situational behaviors, not when they merely intend; and intention without behavioral scaffolding sometimes backfires. Translation: the will succeeds exactly insofar as it stops trying to act on the wanting directly and instead arranges circumstances that act on the wanting for it.

_Clinical psychology_ is built on the same asymmetry. No therapist tells a depressed patient to will themselves into wanting things again — anhedonia is precisely the incapacity of wanting, and exhortation is worse than useless. Behavioral activation works the outside-in route: act first, in small scheduled doses, and let motivation regrow from consequences. Even the cognitive therapies, which sound intellect-first, work by repeated _practice_ that slowly rewires appraisal — Augustine's habit-chain run in reverse, again.

**So the secular translation of "grace," fully unpacked, is something like this:** the motivational core is not directly steerable by its owner; it is reshaped by things that come at it from outside the executive loop — relationships, communities, crises, environments, repeated embodied practice, and occasionally sudden reorganizing experiences whose timing you don't control. The will's real job is indirect: _positioning_. You can't make the wind change, but you can move your boat to where different winds blow — join the community, remove the cues, adopt the practice, put yourself in the way of the experience. Augustine did exactly this after his conversion, and notably, medieval spiritual practice institutionalized it: you cannot compel grace, but you can dispose yourself toward it — prayer, community, liturgy as positioning behavior. Strip the theology and that's contemporary behavior-change science almost clause for clause.

**Where the sixteen-century claim needs honest qualification:** Augustine's strongest version — that the will contributes _nothing_, that grace is wholly prior — outruns the evidence. The modern picture is interactionist: executive effort genuinely matters at the margins, in positioning, persistence, and consolidation; Hudson's subjects really did change on purpose, if indirectly. So the durable core is not "the will is impotent" but the narrower, stranger claim: _the will has no direct write-access to the wants that power it, only indirect access through action and circumstance_ — and cultures that forget this (whether Pelagian monks or willpower-based self-help) keep rediscovering it the hard way. Pelagius, it turns out, was wrong in an interesting way: not because effort doesn't matter, but because effort aimed directly at the wanting misses the mechanism.

For the vault, this is a genuine genealogy node with plural descent lines, exactly the branching structure we discussed: Augustine's divided will → Schopenhauer/Nietzsche (will as non-sovereign) → James's _Varieties_ → two modern branches, the clinical one (AA, behavioral activation, motivational interviewing) and the research one (Berridge's wanting/liking, social investment theory, Hudson's volitional change).
